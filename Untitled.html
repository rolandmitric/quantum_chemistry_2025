Spin from Abstract Vector Spaces to Magnetic Moment#

In this chapter we develop the concept of spin as it emerges naturally from the structure of a two-dimensional complex vector space. Only at the end do we attach its physical meaning via the magnetic moment, which provides the experimental interface between the abstract algebra and measurable laboratory phenomena.

1. The Abstract Two-Dimensional Complex Vector Space#

We begin with a complex vector space \(V \cong \mathbb{C}^2\).
Vectors are written as kets \(|v\rangle \in V\), and linear functionals as bras \(\langle w| \in V^\*\). A Hermitian inner product defines orthogonality and normalization.

Linear operators \(\hat{A}:V\to V\) act on kets via matrix multiplication.
We postpone any physical interpretation until the algebra of transformations reveals its structure.

2. Rotations and the Group SU(2)#

Spatial rotations in three dimensions form the group SO(3). Its double cover, SU(2), has a distinguished two-dimensional representation acting on \(V\). The key idea is that SU(2) acts as internal rotations on the abstract vectors \(|v\rangle\).

A rotation of angle \(\theta\) about a unit vector \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\) is represented by the operator

\[ U(\theta,\hat{\mathbf{n}}) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{i}{2}\,\theta\, \hat{\mathbf{n}}\cdot\boldsymbol{\sigma}\right), \]

where \(\boldsymbol{\sigma}=(\sigma_x,\sigma_y,\sigma_z)\) are the Pauli matrices, introduced solely as generators of SU(2).

3. Pauli Matrices as Generators of Rotations#

The Pauli matrices

\[\begin{split} \sigma_x = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \sigma_y = \begin{pmatrix}0 & -i \\ i & 0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \sigma_z = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1\end{pmatrix}, \end{split}\]

satisfy the commutation relations

\[ [\sigma_i,\sigma_j] = 2i\,\varepsilon_{ijk}\sigma_k. \]

This algebra is mathematically identical to the angular-momentum algebra

\[ [J_i, J_j] = i\hbar \varepsilon_{ijk} J_k. \]

Thus, before any physical assignment, the structure of the operators in \(V\) already mirrors the structure of angular momentum.

4. Defining the Spin Operator#

The simplest identification consistent with the algebra is

\[ \hat{\mathbf{S}} = \frac{\hbar}{2}\boldsymbol{\sigma}. \]

By construction, \(\hat{\mathbf{S}}\) transforms under SU(2) exactly like an angular momentum:

\[ U(\theta,\hat{\mathbf{n}})\,\hat{\mathbf{S}}\,U^\dagger(\theta,\hat{\mathbf{n}}) = R(\theta,\hat{\mathbf{n}})\,\hat{\mathbf{S}}, \]

where \(R\) is the corresponding SO(3) rotation matrix.
This is the defining property of an angular momentum observable.

At this point, spin is a purely internal degree of freedom: an irreducible representation of SU(2) realized in a 2-dimensional Hilbert space.

5. Measurement: Eigenstates and Quantization#

The operator \(S_z = \frac{\hbar}{2}\sigma_z\) has eigenvalues

\[ m_s = \pm \frac{\hbar}{2}, \]

with eigenvectors

\[\begin{split} |+\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}1 \\ 0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad |-\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}0 \\ 1\end{pmatrix}. \end{split}\]

This quantization arises not from a physical picture of rotation, but from the algebraic structure of SU(2).

6. The Bridge to Physics: Introducing the Magnetic Moment#

Only now do we connect the abstract operator \(\hat{\mathbf{S}}\) to an experimentally measurable quantity.
A magnetic dipole couples to a magnetic field according to the Zeeman Hamiltonian

\[ \hat{H}_{\mathrm{Z}} = -\boldsymbol{\mu}\cdot\mathbf{B}. \]

For an electron the magnetic moment is proportional to its spin operator:

\[ \boldsymbol{\mu} = - g \frac{e}{2m_e}\,\hat{\mathbf{S}} = - g \mu_B\,\frac{\boldsymbol{\sigma}}{2}, \]

where \(\mu_B\) is the Bohr magneton and \(g \approx 2\).

Thus the magnetic moment is not an independent object; it is the coefficient that couples the abstract spin operator to the electromagnetic field. It is through this coupling that the abstract SU(2) algebra becomes observable.

7. Stern–Gerlach as the Experimental Visualization#

In a Stern–Gerlach experiment, the Hamiltonian

\[ \hat{H} = -\hat{\mu}_z B_z(z) \]

imparts opposite forces on the two eigenstates of \(S_z\).
A spatial splitting emerges because the spin eigenstates couple differently to the magnetic field gradient. This directly visualizes the eigenstructure of the operator defined earlier in purely algebraic terms.

At this point the pedagogical arc is complete:

  1. Abstract vector space

  2. Generators of SU(2)

  3. Spin operator as an internal angular momentum

  4. Magnetic moment as the physical coupling that makes the operator measurable

  5. Stern–Gerlach as the real-world confirmation

8. Summary#

Spin begins as an abstract representation of SU(2) in a two-dimensional vector space.
The Pauli matrices appear as the generators of this representation, giving rise to an internal angular momentum operator \(\hat{\mathbf{S}}\).
Only when we introduce the Zeeman coupling does the magnetic moment emerge as the link between this abstract structure and measurable physical behavior.

This perspective keeps the mathematical development clean while providing a clear conceptual path from abstract linear algebra to concrete experimental physics.
